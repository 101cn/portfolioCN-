INNEX1 ROVER

For context, the Lunabotics challenge was a competition hosted amongst universities in the USA, introduced into the UK in 25-26 by NASA and Caterpillar in hopes to grow it to a worldwide competition. Its main idea is to build a rover that would be on the lunar soil, where it would avoid craters and rocks and dig lunar soil and store it, then deposit it elsewhere. The competition itself is competed through a points system, where each action has a designated amount of points and how it is executed also affects it. For example, full automation awards more points than manual control.

Electrical Architecture

In terms of electronic structure, I designed two isolated systems to prioritize simplicity and reliability: a power domain (upper schematic) and a compute domain (lower schematic). By splitting the architecture across two separate batteries, I avoided the heavy complications and inefficiencies of excessively stepping voltages up or down across the entire rover.

The power domain runs off a high-capacity 6S 22.2V LiPo battery. It handles all high-current loads, including the 4 drivetrain motors, 4 linear actuators, and the 3-phase brushless excavation motor, distributing power through an 80A ANL fuse and dedicated Cytron motor controllers.

The compute domain is powered independently by a 4S 14.8V battery. It utilizes two custom step-down buck converters to provide stable 12V/5A and 5V/10A rails. This ensures reliable, uninterrupted power delivery to the Jetson Nano, LiDAR L1r, dual OAK-D depth cameras, microcontroller, and the GL-iNet router, preventing brownouts during heavy motor loads.

Both domains were built with modularity in mind, using XT90/XT60 terminals and Wago connectors to allow for rapid hot-swapping and maintenance during the competition.

Electrical Safety

Given the volatile nature of the competition, electrical safety and diagnostic resilience were critical. The high-current power domain is protected by a heavy-duty 80A ANL fuse, with a master Emergency Stop (E-Stop) integrated directly into the main circuit. Engaging the E-Stop instantly severs power to all motors and actuators.

Crucially, because the architecture utilizes a dual-battery setup, triggering the E-Stop or blowing the main fuse does not kill the compute domain. The Jetson Nano, LiDAR, and router remain fully powered and online via their isolated 4S battery and 20A inline fuse. This allows for continuous telemetry streaming, camera feeds, and real-time diagnostics even when the rover's physical movement is safely disabled.

Framework Setup

The entire software stack is built on ROS 2 Humble and Ubuntu 22.04. I architected a highly modular system divided into dedicated node packages, ensuring strict separation of concerns between drivetrain control, excavation, and perception.

For autonomy, the rover utilizes Nav2 integrated with an EKF and RTAB-Map SLAM. Depth data from the dual OAK-D cameras is processed through a custom crater-detection pipeline. Simultaneously, the 3D Ouster LiDAR pointcloud is passed through a custom C++ bounds filter to strip out illegal out-of-arena points, before feeding directly into a Nav2 3D Voxel Layer to detect obstacles and rocks.

Software Safety

On the hardware side, I implemented fail-closed motion safety logic at the ROS level. I built a custom velocity gate node acting as a safety interceptor on the command velocity topic. It requires a constant stream of valid heartbeats to remain open; if a command goes stale for more than 200ms, the gate snaps closed and inhibits the drivetrain. This is paired with stall-detection encoder verification to prevent uncontrolled wheel spinning under low traction.

In addition to the physical hardware disconnect, I integrated a dedicated software E-Stop into the ROS 2 architecture. This acts as an immediate remote-kill mechanism accessible directly from the ground control station. When triggered, it publishes a high-priority motion-inhibit signal to the safety bridge, overriding any active autonomous or manual navigation commands and instantly locking down the drivetrain.
